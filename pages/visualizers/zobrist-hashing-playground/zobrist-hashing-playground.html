Zobrist Hashing Playground

One XOR to make a move. The same XOR to undo it.

Chess engines can't afford to re-hash the entire board on every move. Zobrist hashing assigns a random 64-bit key to every (piece, square) pair — the board's hash is just the XOR of all present keys. Making or undoing a move is exactly one or two XOR operations, regardless of board complexity.

♟️ Why This Technique Is Everywhere

⚡

O(1) Incremental Updates

Making a move XORs out the piece's old (piece, square) key and XORs in its new one — exactly 2 operations, regardless of how complex the position is. No full re-hash, ever.

↩️

Self-Inverse Undo

XOR-ing a value with itself cancels it out (a ⊕ a = 0). Applying the exact same XOR operations used to make a move restores the exact previous hash — undo is free, no separate logic needed.

🔁

Transposition Tables

XOR is commutative — the final hash depends only on which (piece, square) pairs are present, never on the order moves were made. This lets engines detect "I've evaluated this exact position before" and reuse cached evaluations, even via a completely different move sequence.

🎲

The Birthday Bound

With 64-bit random keys, collisions become likely only after roughly 2³² (~4 billion) distinct positions have been hashed — far beyond what any real search tree explores. Real engines like Stockfish rely on exactly this margin of safety.
